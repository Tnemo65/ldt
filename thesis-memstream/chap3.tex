\section{Approach Direction}

CA-DQStream implements a four-layer processing pipeline on Apache Flink that separates infrastructure concerns from algorithmic contributions. The system consists of: Layer 1 (Schema Filter and Deduplication) validates completeness and removes duplicates via Flink KeyedState with 7-day TTL; Layer 2 (Memory-Based Streaming Anomaly Detection) processes records through sequential Canary and Complex stages that converge at the MetaAggregator; Layer 3 (Meta-Aggregation) computes 1-minute windowed quality metrics; and Layer 4 (Intelligent Evolution Controller) monitors meta-streams via ADWIN and triggers multi-strategy self-adaptation. The following sections detail the data exploration (Section 3.2), feature engineering (Section 3.3.2), and the four processing layers (Sections 3.3.3--3.3.6). The system requires an offline warmup phase before deployment: a 3-stage sequential funnel (Physical Violation Filter $\rightarrow$ IQR Outlier Removal $\rightarrow$ Verification) cleans the January 2024 training dataset, removing 3.48\% of records to produce an ultra-clean baseline of approximately 2.86M records on which the Online Autoencoder and 4D thresholds are trained.

\section{Data Exploration}

\subsection{Raw Data EDA}

The NYC Yellow Taxi dataset covers 26 months (January 2022 -- February 2024, approximately 10M records). The EDA reveals bimodal fare distributions (modes around \$8--\$12 for short in-borough trips and \$50--\$60 for airport trips), extreme variance across ratecode types (Standard: mean \$15.42, JFK: mean \$52.18, Newark: mean \$68.44), and volume patterns varying 2--3$\times$ between peak and off-peak periods. Data quality issues include 9.1\% NULL rate on passenger\_count and RatecodeID (systematic upstream capture failure), 1.8\% negative fares (voided trips, data entry errors), 1.5\% zero distances (tolls-only, canceled trips), and sporadic GPS errors producing impossible speeds exceeding 100 mph. See Appendix A for complete EDA with statistical summaries and visualizations.

\subsection{Concept Drift Example}

Empirical evidence of concept drift accumulates over the 26-month observation window. Monthly NULL rate shows a gradual upward trend from approximately 4\% to over 10\% by month 26, driven by upstream system degradation. Total violation rate grows from approximately 10\% to over 30\% as fare distributions shift seasonally. Trip volume remains stable, ruling out volume-driven artifacts. Negative fare spikes appear sporadically, often correlated with payment system changes. A January 2024 blizzard caused sudden drift with a 14.2 percentage point anomaly rate increase within 24 hours. These patterns empirically motivate the ADWIN-based drift monitoring in Layer 4 (Section 3.3.6) and the IEC's multi-strategy adaptation framework. Quantitative evidence supporting the need for adaptive thresholds is provided in Figure A.2.

\subsection{Injected Data}

Four synthetic anomaly types are injected to evaluate detection performance: (1) Meter tampering: fare=\$120 for 3 miles in 8 minutes (\$40/mile, individually each feature passes validation); (2) Short-trip fraud: fare=\$80 for 0.5 miles (\$160/mile, impossible regardless of context); (3) Traffic manipulation: 90 minutes for 2 miles with fare=\$45 (speed passes threshold but fare is inflated for distance); (4) Payment inflation: card payment with tip > 50\% of fare (ratio anomaly undetectable by single-feature rules). Three difficulty levels: Easy (extreme deviations, 3--5$\times$ normal), Medium (moderate deviations, 2--3$\times$ normal), Hard (subtle deviations, 1.5--2$\times$ normal). Injection parameters preserve temporal validity, spatial consistency, cyclical awareness, and correlation structure. Validation compares detection rates against ground truth labels. Full injection details are provided in Chapter 5, Experiments 3 and 4.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}

The Online Autoencoder operates on a 21-dimensional enhanced feature vector comprising 15 base features and 6 ratio features. Base features include: 8 raw/computed features (fare\_amount, trip\_distance, duration\_min, speed\_mph, fare\_per\_mile, fare\_per\_minute, total\_amount, passenger\_count), 4 cyclical temporal encodings (hour\_sin, hour\_cos, dow\_sin, dow\_cos), 2 spatial grid encodings (pickup\_grid\_x, pickup\_grid\_y), and 1 log-transformed feature (distance\_log). Ratio features normalize by baseline expectations: fare\_per\_mile\_ratio, implied\_speed\_ratio, fare\_per\_minute\_ratio, tip\_ratio\_normalized, passenger\_density\_ratio, and distance\_log\_ratio. These ratio features are critical: they reduce coefficient of variation by 10--100$\times$ compared to raw features, enabling context-independent anomaly signals that remain comparable across different operational contexts.

Following established practices from NYC TLC taxi feature engineering \citep{nyctlc}, the system computes Haversine distance from pickup/dropoff coordinates, total travel time in minutes, and meter increment patterns (the per-unit billing increment that reflects fare regulation). The cyclical encodings preserve temporal proximity: hour 23 and hour 0 have Euclidean distance of 0.26 on the unit circle, matching the distance between adjacent hours, which is critical for midnight transition anomalies. The spatial grid maps 265 TLC zones to a 4$\times$4 coordinate system (16 cells), preserving geographic locality while ensuring sufficient sample density per cell for reliable threshold estimation. All features should be normalized to zero mean and unit variance via StandardScaler fitted on the ultra-clean training dataset and log-transformed where appropriate (e.g., $\log(1 + \text{trip\_distance})$), eliminating seasonal artifacts where identical fares produce different anomaly scores at different times of year \citep{sklearn_api}.

\textbf{Feature Dimension Reconciliation.} The Online Autoencoder uses a symmetric architecture (34D input $\rightarrow$ 68D hidden $\rightarrow$ 34D output) as implemented in the production codebase (\texttt{src/ml/memstream\_core.py}). The 34D input comprises: 6 raw trip features (trip\_distance, dur\_min, fare\_amount, passenger\_count, total\_amount, speed\_mph), 3 derived ratios (fare\_per\_mile, fare\_per\_minute, fare\_per\_passenger), 3 temporal raw (pickup\_hour, pickup\_dow, is\_weekend), 4 spatial grid (PU/DO grid x/y), 4 normalized ratios (fare\_per\_mile\_norm, fare\_per\_min\_norm, speed\_norm, pax\_per\_mile), 5 cyclic temporal (hour\_sin/cos, dow\_sin/cos, dist\_squared), 5 ratecode one-hot, 1 is\_night, 2 log transforms (log\_fare, log\_distance), and 1 inter-borough. The ablation baselines B1--B4 use sklearn IF on a 21D feature subset (8 raw + 6 ratio + 4 cyclical temporal + 2 spatial + 1 log-transformed = 21D) to isolate the ratio feature contribution.

The ablation baselines (B1--B4) operate on the 21D feature vector below. The Online AE + Memory Module uses the full 34D feature set (documented in Feature Dimension Reconciliation above). This design choice isolates the contribution of ratio features across baselines B1--B4.

\subsection{Layer 1: Baseline Validation}

Layer 1 addresses the Completeness dimension of DAMA-DMBOK through two sub-stages: schema validation and deduplication. Schema validation performs deterministic NULL checks and range validation on all incoming records, leveraging Confluent's Schema Registry for enforcement of data contracts at the entry point of the streaming pipeline \citep{confluent_schema_registry}. Beyond structural schema enforcement, CA-DQStream employs CEL-based (Common Expression Language) data contracts for semantic validation of value ranges, patterns, and cross-field dependencies \citep{confluent_dq_patterns}. This distinction mirrors Grab's approach: syntactic validation (schema conformance) catches structural violations, while semantic validation (value ranges, cross-field consistency) catches logically or semantically invalid records \citep{grab_streaming_dq}. For instance, a record passing schema validation may still fail semantic checks if fare\_amount is negative or if speed exceeds physical limits. Schema evolution is managed through compatibility modes: BACKWARD (new schema accepts old data), FORWARD (old schema accepts new data), and FULL (bidirectional compatibility) \citep{confluent_schema_registry}. Grab's Coban platform defines data quality tests using FlinkSQL-based specifications, enabling declarative rule definitions that are both human-readable and computationally efficient \citep{grab_coban_platform}.

Schema validation performs deterministic NULL checks and range validation on all incoming records:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{passenger\_count IS NOT NULL} and \texttt{passenger\_count $\in$ [1, 9]}
    \item \texttt{RatecodeID IS NOT NULL} and \texttt{RatecodeID $\in$ \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 99\}}
    \item \texttt{VendorID IS NOT NULL} and \texttt{VendorID $\in$ \{1, 2, 6\}}
    \item \texttt{pickup\_datetime IS NOT NULL}, \texttt{dropoff\_datetime IS NOT NULL}
\end{itemize}

Deduplication uses Flink KeyedState with a composite key: (tpep\_pickup\_datetime, PULocationID, fare\_amount, passenger\_count), stored with 7-day TTL via RocksDB backend. Records failing schema validation are emitted to the \texttt{dq-schema-violations} Kafka topic; records passing schema validation proceed to Layer 2.

Scale: Approximately 310,000 records out of 3M (10.1\%) are rejected at Schema Validation, with the dominant rejection being NULL passenger\_count/RatecodeID (9.1\% of records affected by the same upstream data capture issue).

\subsection{Layer 2: Memory-Based Streaming Anomaly Detection}

Layer 2 implements a sequential two-stage anomaly detection pipeline: after Schema Validation, records pass through a lightweight Canary (Business Rules) stage followed by a Complex (ML-based) stage, with results converging at the MetaAggregator. The ML-based anomaly detection combines a Denoising Autoencoder with a Memory Module and FIFO buffer to detect anomalies in data streams without requiring labeled data. The Memory Module maintains a FIFO circular buffer of 50,000 slots per neighborhood (10 neighborhoods total), each storing the output of the autoencoder encoder at 34 dimensions. On each incoming record, the encoded representation is compared against the K=10 nearest neighbors in memory via L1 (Manhattan) distance, and the gradient is detached to prevent memory updates from backpropagating into the autoencoder. The scoring mechanism computes the anomaly score as:

\begin{equation}
\text{Score}(\mathbf{z}_t) = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \gamma^{i-1} \, R(\mathbf{z}_t, \hat{\mathbf{z}}_i^t)
\label{eq:anomaly_score}
\end{equation}

where $R$ denotes the L1 distance between the encoded incoming observation $\mathbf{z}_t$ and the $i$-th nearest neighbor $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_i^t$ from memory, $\gamma=0.0$ means the most recent neighbor (i=1) dominates the score since $\gamma^i=0$ for all $i>1$ when $\gamma=0$, and $K=10$ nearest neighbors are considered. The Memory Module update policy is conditional: only records with anomaly score below the per-context threshold $\beta$ are used to update memory, preventing memory poisoning from anomalous data. ADWIN is used for concept drift detection within the memory module, enabling the system to recognize when stored patterns no longer reflect current data distribution.

\textbf{Canary Stage (Rule-Based):} Six deterministic business rules evaluate validity and consistency constraints:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{fare\_amount $\geq$ 0} (negative fare: voided trips, data entry errors)
    \item \texttt{trip\_distance $>$ 0} (zero distance: tolls-only, canceled trips)
    \item \texttt{dropoff\_datetime $>$ pickup\_datetime} (impossible timestamp: system errors)
    \item \texttt{speed\_mph $<$ 100} (GPS/meter malfunction)
    \item \texttt{fare\_amount $\leq$ 500} (unreasonably high: meter tampering)
    \item \texttt{payment\_type$\neq$1 $\to$ tip\_amount = 0} (payment inconsistency)
\end{itemize}

The Canary stage processes each record in under 5ms, acting as a lightweight sanity check. Records flagged by Canary are immediately routed to the DLQ/output without entering the ML scoring stage, reducing average processing time by 6.75ms per record.

\textbf{Complex Stage (ML-Based):} The Online Autoencoder with Memory Module scores multivariate anomalies. The model architecture is a symmetric Denoising Autoencoder: 34D input $\rightarrow$ 68D hidden $\rightarrow$ 34D output, with ReLU activation and Gaussian noise injection (std=0.1) during training. The Memory Module stores 50,000 encoded normal patterns per neighborhood in a FIFO circular buffer, scoring via K-nearest neighbors (K=10) with L1 distance on the 34D encoded output space.

Scoring mechanism: For each incoming record $\mathbf{x}$: (1) normalize: apply StandardScaler fitted on ultra-clean training data; (2) encode: $\mathbf{z} = \text{encoder}(\mathbf{x}_{\text{norm}})$ using the AE encoder output at 34D; (3) score: compute L1 kNN distance to K=10 nearest neighbors in the Memory Module; (4) normalize: divide by the per-context beta threshold from ContextBeta; (5) anomaly if ratio $\geq 1.0$. Note: reconstruction error is NOT used in the scoring formula; the anomaly score is the sum of L1 distances to the K nearest neighbors in memory, with gamma=0.0 (most recent neighbor dominates).

\textbf{ContextBeta Thresholding:} The anomaly score is compared against the 4D context-aware threshold matrix. Each cell in the 4D matrix (trip\_type $\times$ time\_window $\times$ day\_type $\times$ neighborhood\_id, 588 cells total) stores the 95th percentile of anomaly scores from the ultra-clean training dataset for that cell. The percentile-based threshold $T_{\text{cell}} = P_{95}(\text{scores}_{\text{cell}})$ is a non-parametric empirical cutoff: it defines the anomaly threshold as the score value below which 95\% of training records fall, naturally targeting a 5\% false positive rate per cell. This approach makes no distributional assumption about the anomaly scores, unlike parametric methods that assume normality. Cells with fewer than 100 samples fall back to the global 95th percentile $T_{\text{global}} = P_{95}(\text{all scores}) = \$45.46$ to avoid overfitting to noise.

\textbf{Fault Tolerance:} The sequential pipeline provides graceful degradation: if the Complex (ML) stage is unavailable, Canary continues processing uninterrupted, and records are handled via fallback thresholds without entering the ML scoring stage. The MetaAggregator synchronizes the two stages via MapState with 5-second TTL, handling out-of-order arrivals.

PyFlink Vectorized UDFs process records in mini-batches (batch\_size=500), achieving 16$\times$ throughput improvement over scalar Python UDFs by amortizing JVM-Python IPC overhead and enabling NumPy's BLAS-accelerated operations.

\subsection{Layer 3: Meta-Aggregation}

The MetaAggregator serves two functions: data routing and meta-stream generation. After Canary and Complex complete their evaluations, results converge at the MetaAggregator, which emits clean records and violation records to appropriate Kafka topics and MinIO buckets. ADWIN-U (KAIS'25) provides the reference implementation for the meta-aggregation layer, comparing a reference window against the current window using unsupervised divergence metrics \citep{adwin2025}. Crucially, higher-order statistics such as Skewness and Kurtosis outperform traditional mean and variance for unsupervised drift detection, as they capture distributional shape changes that first-order statistics miss \citep{adwin2025}. The BAR metric (Balanced Accuracy by Requested Labeled Data) is used when labeled feedback is available to calibrate the meta-aggregation thresholds \citep{adwin2025}. 

Confluent's data quality feedback loop provides the operational model for the MetaAggregator: validation results feed into a quarantine mechanism, monitored via dashboards with threshold alerting, triggering corrective action when quality degrades \citep{confluent_dq_feedback_loop}. The five KPIs tracked by CA-DQStream's meta-aggregation align with industry practice: completeness (proportion of valid records), accuracy (proximity to true values), freshness (event arrival lag vs. processing lag), error rates (violation frequency), and quarantine volume (records held for review) \citep{confluent_dq_feedback_loop}.

The quality meta-stream consists of six metrics computed over 1-minute windows grouped by neighborhood:

\begin{align}
\text{null\_rate}(t) &= \frac{|\{\,\text{NULL records at }t\,\}|}{|\{\,\text{total records at }t\,\}|} \label{eq:null_rate} \\[6pt]
\text{violation\_rate}(t) &= \frac{|\{\,\text{BR violations at }t\,\}|}{|\{\,\text{total records at }t\,\}|} \label{eq:violation_rate} \\[6pt]
\text{anomaly\_rate}(t) &= \frac{|\{\,\text{AE anomalies at }t\,\}|}{|\{\,\text{total records at }t\,\}|} \label{eq:anomaly_rate} \\[6pt]
\text{volume}(t) &= |\{\,\text{records processed at }t\,\}| \quad \text{per minute} \label{eq:volume} \\[6pt]
\text{dedup\_rate}(t) &= \frac{|\{\,\text{duplicates detected at }t\,\}|}{|\{\,\text{total at }t\,\}|} \label{eq:dedup_rate} \\[6pt]
\Delta\_\text{score}(t) &= \frac{\big|\,\text{violation\_rate}(t) - \text{anomaly\_rate}(t)\,\big|}
                               {\text{violation\_rate}(t) + \text{anomaly\_rate}(t) + \epsilon} \label{eq:delta_score}
\end{align}

The $\Delta$\_score (Canary-Complex Divergence) measures normalized disagreement between rule-based and ML-based anomaly classifications. When $\Delta$\_score increases, the ML model's learned patterns no longer align with current data distribution, signaling concept uncertainty that ADWIN monitors.

\begin{longtable}{|m{2.5cm}|M{2cm}|m{2.5cm}|m{5.5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{$\delta$} & \textbf{Aggregation} & \textbf{Self-Adaptation Action} \\ \hline
null\_rate & 0.002 & 1-minute & Recalculate schema validation thresholds \\ \hline
violation\_rate & 0.002 & 1-minute & Recalculate business rule thresholds \\ \hline
anomaly\_rate & 0.001 & 1-minute & Reset Online AE memory / trigger warmup \\ \hline

\textbf{$\Delta$\_score} & 0.001 & 5-minute & Reset AE memory or trigger warmup on fresh clean data \\ \hline

\caption{ADWIN parameters for IEC drift detection.}
\label{tab:adwin_params}
\end{longtable}

The six metrics are emitted to the \texttt{dq-meta-stream} Kafka topic and fed to ADWIN in Layer 4.

\subsection{Layer 4: Intelligent Evolution Controller}

The Intelligent Evolution Controller (IEC) monitors quality meta-streams via ADWIN and triggers multi-strategy self-adaptation when drift is detected. The IEC classifies drift into five scenarios based on meta-stream trends. METER (MEta-learning-based adapTER, arXiv:2312.16831) \citep{meter_2023} uses an MLP hypernetwork (64-32-16 architecture) trained on historical drift patterns to predict optimal adaptation parameters from current meta-metrics, providing the algorithmic foundation for moderate drift adaptation where detection boundaries shift but overall model structure remains. DTD (Dynamic Threshold Discovery) \citep{dtd_2026} formally proves that no single fixed threshold is universally optimal, and introduces a 3-way comparison framework: (1) old model retains too late (drift is missed), (2) new model applies at correct timing (optimal adaptation), (3) false alarm (no drift present, switching would be harmful). The SCAR benchmark (Streaming Concept Adaptation with Reconstruction, Artificial Intelligence Review, 2025) \citep{scar_2025} establishes that periodic retraining (reconstruction strategy) is a surprisingly strong baseline, validating CA-DQStream's continuous evolution approach. ADWIN (Adaptive Windowing, SIAM'07) \citep{bifet2007learning} provides the core drift detection mechanism: when the mean difference between a reference window and a current window exceeds a statistically computed threshold $\varepsilon$, drift is declared. The IEC monitors three key signals for adaptation decisions: Importance (drift magnitude), Error (detection rate degradation), and Correctness (false positive rate increase), coordinating these signals to select the optimal adaptation strategy.

\begin{longtable}{|m{3cm}|m{4.5cm}|m{3cm}|m{3cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Scenario} & \textbf{Condition} & \textbf{Interpretation} & \textbf{Strategy} \\ \hline
\textbf{IDEAL\_STATE} & $\Delta_{\text{null}} < 0$ AND $\Delta_{\text{viol}} < 0$ AND $\Delta_{\text{anom}} < 0$ & All quality metrics improving & No action (monitor) \\ \hline
\textbf{DATA\_QUALITY\_CRISIS} & $\Delta_{\text{null}} > 0.05$ OR $\Delta_{\text{viol}} > 0.05$ & Upstream data source degradation & Alert operators (not model issue) \\ \hline
\textbf{SUDDEN\_DRIFT} & $\Delta_{\text{viol}} < 0.01$ AND $\Delta_{\text{anom}} > 0.1$ & Rules stable but ML detects new anomalies & Switching Scheme (swap to recent model) \\ \hline
\textbf{MODEL\_BLINDNESS} & $\Delta_{\text{viol}} > 0.05$ AND $\Delta_{\text{anom}} < 0.01$ & Rules catch violations but ML misses & Continuous Evolution (retrain on recent data) \\ \hline
\textbf{INCREMENTAL\_DRIFT} & $0.01 < \Delta_{\text{anom}} < 0.1$ & Gradual distribution shift & METER Adaptation (parameter adjustment) \\ \hline
\caption{IEC drift scenario classification and strategy mapping.}
\label{tab:iec_scenarios}
\end{longtable}

Four adaptation strategies are available, applied in sequential fallback order:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Continuous Evolution:} When ADWIN detects drift in the anomaly rate meta-stream, the Memory Module resets and refills via FIFO replacement: normal records (score below threshold) are encoded and appended to the FIFO buffer, progressively replacing the oldest entries. The autoencoder weights remain frozen during this phase; only the memory content is refreshed. This avoids catastrophic forgetting while adapting to new normal patterns. Used for gradual drift with slow metric changes (e.g., seasonal fare increase). Cost: seconds, no downtime.

    \item \textbf{Switching Scheme:} Maintain pre-trained model ensemble (weekday model, weekend model, seasonal models) and switch based on temporal context. Used for recurring drift with cyclic patterns (e.g., weekend vs. weekday traffic). Cost: negligible (model swap via Broadcast State).

    \item \textbf{METER Adaptation:} METER (MEta-learning-based adapTER) uses an MLP hypernetwork (64-32-16 architecture) trained on historical drift patterns to predict optimal adaptation parameters from current meta-metrics. Used for moderate drift where detection boundaries shift but overall model structure remains. Cost: 2--5 minutes, adaptation latency while current model continues serving traffic. Note: METER is validated through its allocation of 6\% of drift events (3 out of 54); per-event displacement predictions are not independently benchmarked but METER's contribution is reflected in the overall IEC effectiveness (H3).

    \item \textbf{Spatial Tracking:} Train per-neighborhood models for affected zones, keep global model for others. Used for localized drift affecting specific geographic areas (e.g., construction closes major route). Cost: hours for multi-model training. Rare: only 1\% of drift events.
\end{enumerate}

The IEC executes a 3-tier self-adaptation protocol. Tier 1 (within 1 minute): emit drift alert to Prometheus/Grafana, expand monitoring window. Tier 2 (within 10 minutes): recalculate relevant thresholds via A/B validation, commit if new FPR improves. Tier 3 (within 1 hour): trigger model update (memory reset, METER shift, or full retrain), publish to \texttt{if-model-updates} Kafka topic, deliver to all Flink instances via Broadcast State.

Zero-downtime model swap: the Flink pipeline continues processing records during model updates. The current model serves traffic while the new model downloads asynchronously. An atomic volatile reference swap provides sub-millisecond swap latency with no pipeline interruption.

On model update failures, an Action Replay Worker retries with exponential backoff ($2^n$ seconds, up to 10 retries), with a 15-minute cooldown lock preventing retry storms during sustained outages.

\subsection{Fault Tolerance}

CA-DQStream achieves end-to-end exactly-once semantics through two complementary mechanisms. First, Flink Checkpointing implements Chandy-Lamport distributed snapshots \citep{chandy_lamport_1985}, capturing periodic state snapshots (45-second intervals) to MinIO S3 storage, enabling failure recovery without data loss. Second, Kafka Transactions use transactional writes with \texttt{transaction.timeout.ms = 10 minutes}; on checkpoint recovery, in-flight transactions are aborted and replayed idempotently \citep{confluent_dq_patterns}. The dead-letter queue (DLQ) pattern from Confluent \citep{confluent_dead_letter_queue} handles records that fail validation: invalid records are diverted to a quarantine topic rather than blocking the pipeline. However, practitioners should note that DLQ creates error-free but \textit{incomplete} streams---using DLQ without monitoring leads to silent data loss, where records are removed from the stream but never corrected or reprocessed \citep{confluent_dead_letter_queue}. For atomicity across Kafka and external databases, CA-DQStream employs two-phase commit (2PC) coordination, ensuring that either all operations in a transaction commit or none do. Idempotent operations are used throughout via upsert modes with unique keys, allowing safe reprocessing of records after failures without creating duplicates \citep{confluent_dq_patterns}.

State size is carefully managed: the deduplication operator stores Boolean flags (not full records) per trip\_id within the 7-day TTL window, approximately 56 MB per slot with RocksDB overhead. Bloom filters reduce RocksDB I/O by 90\%. The MetaAggregator uses the AggregateFunction pattern to prevent OOM on large windows.

Model distribution: PyFlink executes Python UDFs in separate worker processes per TaskManager slot. Broadcast State distributes model parameters and memory state across these workers for in-process scoring with microsecond access latency. A 4-node cluster generates approximately 768 MB aggregate network traffic per model update, with approximately 150--200ms GC pause on each receiving TaskManager (G1GC, 4GB heap). With update frequency of 2--3 times/day, total GC overhead is less than 0.01\% of daily uptime.

The pipeline uses 7 Kafka topics with 12 partitions each: \texttt{taxi-nyc-raw} (raw NYC TLC records), \texttt{dq-schema-violations}, \texttt{dq-hard-rule-violations}, \texttt{dq-if-anomalies}, \texttt{dq-anomaly-scores}, \texttt{dq-meta-stream}, and \texttt{if-model-updates}.

MinIO stores all pipeline outputs via Flink StreamingFileSink in Parquet format (columnar compression provides 3--5$\times$ storage reduction over row-based formats): \texttt{cadqstream-raw}, \texttt{cadqstream-violations}, \texttt{cadqstream-anomalies}, \texttt{cadqstream-metrics}, \texttt{cadqstream-drift}, \texttt{cadqstream-checkpoints}, and \texttt{mlflow-artifacts}.

\section{Chapter Summary}

This chapter presented the CA-DQStream framework, a context-aware streaming data quality monitoring system built on four core architectural innovations. The 4D Context-Aware Thresholding framework partitions the data space into 588 context cells, reducing false positive rates compared to global thresholds, with particular benefit for high-value data segments where global thresholds catastrophically fail. The Sequential Processing Pipeline implements a lightweight Canary (Business Rules) stage that filters records before expensive ML scoring, followed by a Complex (Online Autoencoder + Memory Module) stage, with results converging at the MetaAggregator for improved throughput compared to a single-stage ML-only pipeline. The Intelligent Evolution Controller with four adaptation strategies maintains model accuracy under concept drift, preferring lightweight incremental updates to full retraining for computational efficiency. The Online Autoencoder with Memory Module detects multivariate anomalies that business rules cannot capture, with continuous memory-based learning that adapts to evolving distributions.

The experimental evaluation in Chapter 5 validates these contributions through comprehensive ablation studies and comparative baselines, demonstrating that CA-DQStream achieves production-grade data quality monitoring with context awareness, low latency, high throughput, and autonomous adaptation to concept drift.
