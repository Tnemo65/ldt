\section{Approach Direction}

CA-DQStream implements a four-layer processing pipeline on Apache Flink. Layer 1 (Schema Filter and Deduplication) acts as a deterministic quality gate. Layer 2 (Memory-Based Streaming Anomaly Detection) processes records through sequential Canary and Complex stages that converge at the MetaAggregator (Section 3.3.5). Layer 3 (Meta-Aggregation) computes 1-minute windowed quality metrics. Layer 4 (Intelligent Evolution Controller) monitors meta-streams via ADWIN and triggers drift-adaptive model retraining via the \texttt{quick\_retrain} action. The following sections detail the data exploration (Section 3.2), feature engineering (Section 3.3.3.2), the four processing layers (Sections 3.3.3--3.3.6), and fault tolerance (Section 3.3.7). The system requires an offline warmup phase before deployment, detailed in Section 3.3.3.5.

\section{Data Exploration}

\subsection{Raw Data EDA}

The NYC Yellow Taxi dataset covers 26 months (January 2024 -- February 2026, approximately 72M records). The EDA reveals bimodal fare distributions (modes around \$8--\$12 for short in-borough trips and \$50--\$60 for airport trips), extreme variance across ratecode types (Standard: mean \$15.42, JFK: mean \$52.18, Newark: mean \$68.44), and volume patterns varying 2--3$\times$ between peak and off-peak periods. Data quality issues include 9.1\% NULL rate on passenger\_count and RatecodeID (systematic upstream capture failure), 1.8\% negative fares (voided trips, data entry errors), 1.5\% zero distances (tolls-only, canceled trips), and sporadic GPS errors producing impossible speeds exceeding 100 mph.

\textbf{Schema and Completeness Violations:} The raw parquet files contain 20 columns; 5 additional features (duration\_s, speed\_mph, hour, month, weekday, date) are derived in the pipeline. Among raw columns, 5 exhibit NULL values (Table~\ref{tab:eda_null}). The columns passenger\_count, RatecodeID, store\_and\_f\_flag, congestion\_surcharge, and Airport\_fee share an identical NULL count of 11,611,894 records (23.83\% of the dataset), indicating a systematic upstream capture failure affecting a consistent subset of data uploads. The derived column speed\_mph has 544,069 NULL values (1.12\%) attributable to uncomputable trips where either trip\_distance or duration\_s is zero. Beyond NULLs, out-of-range values appear in categorical fields: PULocationID exceeds the valid range (1--263) in 103,043 records (0.21\%); DOLocationID in 370,258 records (0.90\%); and passenger\_count contains 260,062 out-of-range values (0.53\%).

\textbf{Physical Plausibility Violations:} Speed outliers reach physically impossible values: 13,700 records exceed 80 mph, 12,242 exceed 100 mph, and 9,021 exceed 200 mph, with a maximum recorded speed of 5,301,620.40 mph (clearly a GPS or GPS-to-meter data corruption). \subsection{Concept Drift Example}

Empirical evidence of concept drift accumulates over the 26-month observation window. Monthly NULL rate shows a gradual upward trend from approximately 4\% to over 10\% by month 26, driven by upstream system degradation. Total violation rate grows from approximately 10\% to over 30\% as fare distributions shift seasonally. Trip volume remains stable, ruling out volume-driven artifacts. Negative fare spikes appear sporadically, often correlated with payment system changes. A January 2024 blizzard caused sudden drift with a 14.2 percentage point anomaly rate increase within 24 hours. These patterns empirically motivate the ADWIN-based drift monitoring in Layer 4 (Section 3.3.6) and the IEC's drift-adaptive retraining framework.

\textbf{Dataset Composition.} The 26-month evaluation window consists of two components: the earlier period contains real TLC Yellow Taxi trip records, while the later period contains synthetic records generated by a statistical generative model trained on the real data distribution. The synthetic data preserves the statistical properties (marginal distributions, inter-feature correlations, temporal patterns) of the real period while injecting controlled anomaly signals at known rates for evaluation ground truth. Full generation methodology and validation are detailed in Chapter 5.

\subsection{Injected Data}

To evaluate detection performance, several synthetic anomaly types are injected into the dataset. Here is a breakdown of these anomalies:

\textbf{Meter tampering:} A high fare is recorded for a relatively short distance completed in a brief amount of time. The sophistication lies in the fact that, when examined individually, each feature appears perfectly valid and easily passes standard validation checks.

\textbf{Short-trip fraud:} The fare is excessively inflated for an extremely short distance. This results in a fare-per-distance ratio that is completely illogical and impossible regardless of the context.

\textbf{Traffic manipulation:} The travel time is unusually prolonged for a short distance, which leads to an inflated fare. Although the vehicle's speed remains within acceptable thresholds, the final fare is unjustifiably high for the actual distance traveled.

\textbf{Payment inflation:} Card payments feature a tip that constitutes a disproportionately large percentage of the total fare. This ratio anomaly is highly concealed and remains completely undetectable if the system only uses single-feature validation rules.

Three difficulty levels are defined: Easy (extreme deviations, 3--5$\times$ normal), Medium (moderate deviations, 2--3$\times$ normal), Hard (subtle deviations, 1.5--2$\times$ normal). Injection parameters preserve temporal validity, spatial consistency, cyclical awareness, and correlation structure. Validation compares detection rates against ground truth labels. Full injection details are provided in Chapter 5, Experiments 3 and 4.

\section{Proposed Method}

\subsection{Overview}

Figure~\ref{fig:system_architecture} illustrates the end-to-end architecture of CA-DQStream. The system consists of five interconnected components: a message broker that decouples data ingestion from processing, a stream processing engine that executes the four-layer pipeline, an object store that persists trained models and evaluation artifacts, a metrics collector that tracks pipeline health, and a visualization dashboard that enables real-time monitoring and debugging.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Message Broker (Kafka).} Raw taxi trip records are continuously published to a message broker topic as they arrive from the upstream data source. The message broker serves as the ingestion backbone for three reasons: it provides durable buffering so that the processing engine never misses data during temporary downstream delays or restarts; it enables the pipeline to be scaled horizontally by allowing multiple processing instances to consume from the same topic in parallel; and it naturally segments the stream into replayable partitions that preserve temporal ordering within each partition. Schema violations and hard-rule violations detected at each layer are emitted to separate dedicated topics, keeping the main processing path clean and enabling downstream inspection of failures without blocking the throughput path.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Stream Processing Engine (Flink).} All four processing layers run inside the Flink cluster. Flink provides exactly-once state consistency, which means each record is processed once and state updates are atomic even during machine failures. The cluster consists of a coordinator that schedules work and multiple worker nodes that execute the pipeline operators in parallel.

The four processing layers execute in a linear pipeline within Flink. Each record passes through all four layers in sequence:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Layer 1 -- Schema Filter and Deduplication.} Each incoming record first undergoes structural validation against the registered schema, which checks that all required fields are present and have the correct data types. Records failing structural validation are immediately routed to a dedicated violations topic for later inspection without consuming further processing resources. Records passing structural checks then enter the deduplication stage, which checks whether an identical record has been seen recently, using a time-bounded in-memory cache to avoid processing duplicates that arise from upstream retries. Records confirmed as unique proceed downstream.

    \item \textbf{Layer 2 -- Memory-Based Streaming Anomaly Detection.} Each deduplicated record is evaluated by a two-stage detector. The first stage applies a small set of fast deterministic rules that catch obviously impossible records such as negative fares, zero travel distances, or physically impossible speeds. Records that pass these rules enter the second stage, which encodes the record into a learned feature space using a neural network trained during the offline warmup phase. The encoded representation is compared against a buffer of recently observed normal patterns using a nearest-neighbor distance metric. Records whose distance from the normal pattern buffer exceeds a context-specific threshold are flagged as anomalous; records passing all checks are inserted into the normal pattern buffer for future comparisons. The anomaly decision and metadata are emitted downstream.

    \item \textbf{Layer 3 -- Meta-Aggregation.} Anomalous records and clean records are aggregated separately. Each minute, the aggregator computes summary statistics over the preceding window: the rate of hard-rule violations, the rate of ML-detected anomalies, the rate of NULL values across fields, the total record volume, and the average anomaly score among flagged records. These six metrics capture the current state of data quality and are emitted as a single summary record to the meta-stream topic.

    \item \textbf{Layer 4 -- Intelligent Evolution Controller.} The meta-stream metrics are consumed by a drift monitoring component that continuously compares recent statistical properties of each quality metric against the historical baseline. When the statistical divergence exceeds a configurable threshold, the controller declares a concept drift event and evaluates whether the current anomaly detection thresholds and the neural network model remain calibrated for the shifted data distribution. If recalibration is needed, the controller triggers a retraining action, which updates the model parameters and broadcasts the refreshed model to all Flink workers so that subsequent records use the updated detector.
\end{itemize}

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Object Store (MinIO).} The object store holds two types of artifacts. First, it stores the trained neural network model files produced during the offline warmup phase and during retraining events triggered by Layer 4. Second, it stores periodic snapshots of the anomaly detection state---the threshold matrix and the current normal pattern buffer---so that the system can recover to a known consistent state after a planned maintenance restart or an unplanned failure without losing the accumulated behavioral knowledge.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Metrics Collector (Prometheus).} Every component of the pipeline exposes operational metrics in a standard exposition format: record counts at each layer, processing latency percentiles, the number of anomalies detected per minute, the current drift detection status, memory buffer utilization, and model version. Prometheus scrapes these endpoints periodically and stores the time-series data, enabling alerting when any metric crosses a configured threshold.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Visualization Dashboard (Grafana).} The Grafana dashboard connects to Prometheus and renders the operational metrics as real-time charts. Dashboards are organized by layer, showing throughput and latency for the stream processing stages, anomaly detection performance over time, and drift detection signals. Alerting rules defined in Grafana send notifications when drift is detected, when the anomaly rate spikes unexpectedly, or when processing latency exceeds targets.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Offline Training Layer.} Before the streaming pipeline starts, an offline training phase prepares the neural network and the threshold matrix using a curated historical dataset of clean, representative trip records. The training phase proceeds in three steps: first, a denoising autoencoder learns to reconstruct normal trip patterns, producing a compact latent representation that captures the typical structure of valid records; second, the trained encoder processes all training records to produce encoded representations that are used to compute per-context thresholds as the ninety-fifth percentile of normal reconstruction distances; third, the encoder weights, the normalization parameters, and the threshold matrix are serialized and published to the object store, where the streaming pipeline retrieves them at startup. The offline phase also produces the initial contents of the normal pattern buffer by processing a warmup window of clean records through the trained encoder.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{End-to-End Flow and Scenarios.} The complete record lifecycle proceeds as follows. A raw trip record arrives from the upstream source and is published to the Kafka topic. Flink consumes the record and executes Layer 1: the record passes schema validation (otherwise it is sent to the violations topic and dropped from the main path), then deduplication checks confirm it is not a duplicate (duplicates are silently dropped). The record passes to Layer 2: the Canary rules evaluate fast physical constraints (negative fare, zero distance, impossible speed); records failing any rule are sent to the violations topic and the pipeline continues. Passing records are encoded by the neural network, scored against the normal pattern buffer, and compared to the context-specific threshold. If anomalous, the record is flagged and included in the meta-aggregator's anomaly count; if normal, it is inserted into the pattern buffer. Layer 3 aggregates counts and scores over one-minute windows and emits six quality metrics to the meta-stream topic. Layer 4 consumes the meta-stream, maintains sliding statistical windows of each metric, and compares recent distributions against the historical baseline. If a mean shift, variance change, skewness shift, or kurtosis shift is statistically detected, the controller evaluates whether the current model and thresholds remain valid. If they do not, it triggers a retraining action and broadcasts the updated model to all Flink workers, which reload from the object store.

Three failure scenarios illustrate the system's resilience. First, if an upstream system temporarily sends malformed records, Layer 1 rejects them at the schema gate without disrupting downstream processing. Second, if the data distribution gradually shifts (for example, an upstream system begins omitting a field more frequently), Layer 4 detects the statistical change in the meta-stream and triggers model retraining before the anomaly detector's accuracy degrades significantly. Third, if a Flink worker node fails, Flink's checkpointing mechanism restores all state (including the normal pattern buffer and ADWIN windows) from the last consistent snapshot stored in the object store, resuming processing without data loss or duplicate processing.

The system requires an offline warmup phase before deployment, detailed in Section 3.3.3.5.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}

The Online Autoencoder operates on a \textbf{34-dimensional} feature vector extracted from NYC Yellow Taxi trip records. Features are organized into eight categories:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Raw trip features (6D)}: Direct measurements from the trip record.
    \item \textbf{Derived ratios (3D)}: Pairwise ratios of raw features that normalize for scale.
    \item \textbf{Temporal raw (3D)}: Hour, day-of-week, and weekend flag.
    \item \textbf{Spatial grid (4D)}: Pickup and dropoff zone coordinates mapped to a 16$\times$17 grid.
    \item \textbf{Normalized ratios (4D)}: Ratios divided by empirical denominators to reduce variance.
    \item \textbf{Cyclic + nonlinear (5D)}: Sinusoidal encodings of time plus distance squared.
    \item \textbf{Ratecode one-hot (5D)}: Binary encoding of the five fare rate codes.
    \item \textbf{Miscellaneous (4D)}: is\_night, log transforms, and inter-borough indicator (4 features).
\end{itemize}

The full 34D feature vector (Table~\ref{tab:feature_vector}) is normalized via StandardScaler fitted on the ultra-clean training dataset. Ratio features are critical: they reduce the coefficient of variation by 10--100$\times$ compared to raw features, enabling context-independent anomaly signals that remain comparable across different operational contexts (e.g., a \$50 fare is normal for JFK but anomalous for a 0.5-mile in-borough trip). Cyclical encodings preserve temporal proximity: hour 23 and hour 0 have Euclidean distance of 0.26 on the unit circle, matching the distance between adjacent hours, which is critical for midnight transition anomalies.

\textbf{Feature Dimension Reconciliation.} The ablation baselines B1--B4 use sklearn IsolationForest on a reduced feature set to isolate the contribution of ratio features and the autoencoder. The ablation uses 21 features (rows 1--16, 21, 26--30 in Table~\ref{tab:feature_vector}: 6 raw + 3 derived ratios + 3 temporal + 4 spatial + 5 ratecode one-hot), demonstrating that ratio features and cyclic/nonlinear features contribute incrementally to detection performance. The ablation does not test individual features within these groups; the 21-feature set is treated as the minimum viable feature set. The 4D context-aware thresholding framework (Section 3.3.4.3) operates on the full 34D input.

\subsection{Layer 1: Baseline Validation}

Layer 1 addresses the Completeness dimension of the DAMA-DMBOK data quality framework \citep{dama2017dmbok} through two processing substages: schema validation and deduplication. This layer implements a deterministic quality gate that enforces structural and semantic contracts at the entry point of the streaming pipeline, preventing invalid records from propagating downstream to expensive ML-based processing stages. The separation of lightweight schema validation from heavyweight anomaly detection aligns with established streaming data quality best practices, where quick-fail mechanisms reduce computational waste and improve overall pipeline efficiency \citep{confluent_dq_patterns}.

\subsubsection{Schema Validation}

Schema validation enforces the Completeness quality dimension by rejecting records that fail structural or semantic checks. CA-DQStream employs Confluent Schema Registry as the central enforcement mechanism, providing versioned schema management with AVRO serialization for type-safe data contracts \citep{confluent_schema_registry}. The registry stores all schema versions with configurable compatibility modes, enabling safe schema evolution without pipeline disruption.

\textbf{Schema Compatibility Modes.} Schema evolution is governed by three compatibility strategies \citep{confluent_schema_registry}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{BACKWARD compatibility}: New schema versions can read data written under older schemas. Consumer applications using the new schema can process existing data, enabling seamless rolling upgrades where producers adopt new schemas before consumers.
    \item \textbf{FORWARD compatibility}: Old schema versions can read data written under newer schemas. Producer applications can adopt new schemas before all consumers have upgraded, providing a safety buffer for consumer migration.
    \item \textbf{FULL compatibility}: Bidirectional compatibility ensuring both BACKWARD and FORWARD guarantees. Either schema version can read data written under the other, providing maximum flexibility at the cost of stricter validation constraints.
\end{itemize}

For the NYC TLC dataset, CA-DQStream employs BACKWARD compatibility mode, enabling the pipeline to accept new schema variants while maintaining consumer-side stability. The schema evolution strategy ensures that producer-side schema changes (e.g., adding new optional fields) do not invalidate downstream consumers.

\textbf{Data Contract Specification.} Beyond structural schema enforcement, CA-DQStream employs CEL (Common Expression Language) for declarative semantic validation of value ranges, patterns, and cross-field dependencies \citep{confluent_dq_patterns}. This dual-layer approach distinguishes syntactic validation (schema conformance) from semantic validation (value validity), following the taxonomy established by Grab's streaming data quality framework \citep{grab_streaming_dq}. Syntactic validation catches structural violations (missing fields, type mismatches), while semantic validation catches logically invalid records (impossible value combinations, domain constraint violations).

CEL expressions provide a readable, executable specification for data contracts. \texttt{passenger\_count} must be in $[1, 9]$ and non-NULL. \texttt{RatecodeID} must be in $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 99\}$ and non-NULL. \texttt{VendorID} must be in $\{1, 2, 6\}$ and non-NULL.

\textbf{Cross-Field Dependency Validation.} CEL contracts enforce cross-field semantic constraints that individual field validation cannot capture. The following compound constraints encode domain knowledge about valid trip records: fare\_amount $\geq 0$; trip\_distance $> 0$; dropoff\_datetime $>$ pickup\_datetime; and both pickup\_datetime and dropoff\_datetime must be non-NULL. These constraints prevent logically impossible records from entering the pipeline, such as trips with negative fares (voided transactions, data entry errors) or negative distances (GPS errors). The combination of Schema Registry for structural enforcement and CEL contracts for semantic enforcement creates a comprehensive validation barrier at the pipeline entry point.

\textbf{Invalid Record Routing.} Records failing schema validation are emitted to a dedicated Kafka topic for downstream inspection and root cause analysis, ensuring that validation failures do not halt the streaming pipeline.

Empirical results from the evaluation dataset (Section 5.2) reveal that approximately 310,000 records out of 3,000,000 total records (10.1\%) are rejected at schema validation. The dominant rejection cause is NULL passenger\_count and NULL RatecodeID (9.1\% of records), attributable to a systematic upstream data capture failure in the TLC data collection infrastructure.

\subsubsection{Flink KeyedState Deduplication}

Deduplication eliminates duplicate records arising from Kafka consumer rebalancing, producer retries, or upstream system artifacts. CA-DQStream implements deduplication using Flink's KeyedState API with a composite business key, providing exactly-once deduplication semantics within a configurable time window.

\textbf{Composite Key Design.} Deduplication uses a composite key tuple constructed from immutable trip attributes:

\begin{equation}
K_{\text{dedup}} = \langle t_{\text{pickup}}, \text{PULocationID}, \text{fare\_amount}, \text{passenger\_count} \rangle
\label{eq:dedup_key}
\end{equation}

This composite key provides high uniqueness granularity: the combination of pickup timestamp (to the second), pickup zone, fare amount, and passenger count yields near-zero collision probability for genuine distinct trips. The key excludes mutable attributes (dropoff time, trip distance, payment type) that may differ between duplicate submissions of the same trip.

\textbf{RocksDB State Backend.} State persistence uses the RocksDB state backend, which stores KeyedState as sorted key-value pairs on local disk, enabling state growth beyond available heap memory \citep{flink_rocksdb}. RocksDB provides write-ahead logging (WAL) and configurable compaction strategies that balance write throughput against storage efficiency. The state backend stores deduplication flags (Boolean markers) rather than full record copies, minimizing per-key storage overhead.

\textbf{TTL Eviction and State Size.} The deduplication window employs a 7-day TTL enforced via Flink's StateTtlConfig (604,800 seconds):

State size estimation for the deduplication operator:

\begin{align}
N_{\text{max}} &= \text{volume\_per\_day} \times 7 = \frac{3{,}000{,}000}{31} \times 7 \approx 677{,}419 \text{ keys} \label{eq:state_count} \\
S_{\text{key}} &= |K_{\text{dedup}}| = 8 + 4 + 8 + 4 = 24 \text{ bytes} \label{eq:key_size} \\
S_{\text{value}} &= 1 \text{ byte (Boolean flag)} \label{eq:value_size} \\
S_{\text{RocksDB}} &= N_{\text{max}} \times (S_{\text{key}} + S_{\text{value}}) \times \alpha_{\text{rocksdb}} \label{eq:rocksdb_overhead}
\end{align}

where $\alpha_{\text{rocksdb}} \approx 2.3$ accounts for RocksDB's write amplification (B-tree block padding, WAL overhead, SSTable metadata). Substituting:

\begin{equation}
S_{\text{RocksDB}} \approx 677{,}419 \times 25 \times 2.3 \approx 38.9 \text{ MB}
\label{eq:state_size_calc}
\end{equation}

With an additional 30\% overhead for Flink's state management metadata, the total memory footprint is approximately \textbf{56 MB per TaskManager slot}, consistent with the figure cited in Section 3.3.7. This footprint remains constant regardless of throughput scaling, as TTL eviction bounds the maximum state cardinality.

\textbf{Bloom Filter Optimization.} CA-DQStream integrates a Bloom filter as a probabilistic pre-check before KeyedState access, reducing RocksDB I/O operations by approximately 90\% \citep{flink_rocksdb_bloom}. The Bloom filter provides constant-time membership testing with configurable false positive rate:

\begin{align}
P_{\text{false}} &= (1 - e^{-k \cdot n / m})^k \label{eq:bloom_fpr} \\
m &= -\frac{n \cdot \ln P_{\text{false}}}{(\ln 2)^2} \label{eq:bloom_bits}
\end{align}

where $n$ is the expected number of keys (677,419), $k$ is the number of hash functions (optimal: $k = (m/n) \ln 2$), and $m$ is the bit array size. With $P_{\text{false}} = 0.01$, $m \approx 9.8$ Mbits $\approx$ 1.2 MB. Negative lookups eliminate RocksDB access entirely; positive lookups proceed to KeyedState verification.

\begin{figure}[H]
\caption{Bloom filter pre-check skips RocksDB for non-duplicates; positive hits verify in KeyedState.}
\label{alg:dedup}
\begin{lstlisting}[style=pseudocode, language=Python]
# Input: Record r, composite key K=<t,PU,f,p>, BloomFilter B, KeyedState S
# Output: pass (1) or duplicate (0)

# Phase 1: Bloom filter pre-check
if not B.contains(K):
    B.insert(K)          # negative lookup: insert & pass
    return 1
else:
    s = S.get(K)        # RocksDB verification
    if s == NULL:
        S.put(K, TRUE)   # first occurrence: insert with TTL
        return 1
    elif s == TRUE:
        return 0         # duplicate: reject
\end{lstlisting}
\end{figure}

The two-phase lookup (Bloom filter $\rightarrow$ KeyedState) reduces average-case RocksDB I/O by 90\% for workloads with low duplicate rates. For the NYC TLC dataset, duplicate rates below 0.5\% mean that approximately 99.5\% of records receive a negative Bloom filter result, bypassing RocksDB entirely.

\textbf{Dedup Rate Metrics.} The deduplication rate is tracked per 1-minute window:

\begin{equation}
\text{dedup\_rate}(t) = \frac{|\{\,\text{duplicates detected at }t\,\}|}{|\{\,\text{total records at }t\,\}|}
\label{eq:dedup_rate}
\end{equation}

Empirical measurements from the production deployment indicate a deduplication rate below 0.5\% for the NYC TLC dataset, with spikes during Kafka consumer group rebalancing events (up to 2.1\% during rebalance).

\subsubsection{Canary Pattern: Lightweight Validation}

Following schema validation and deduplication, Layer 1 implements a Canary pattern that performs rapid deterministic business rule evaluation on all passing records. The Canary stage serves as a lightweight pre-filter that routes obviously invalid records directly to the violation topic without entering the expensive ML-based anomaly detection stages in Layer 2.

\textbf{Performance Characteristics.} The Canary stage is engineered for minimal latency, targeting sub-5ms per-record processing time:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CPU-bound evaluation}: Six deterministic boolean expressions evaluated in a single pass over the record fields
    \item \textbf{No external dependencies}: No network calls, no state access, no ML inference
    \item \textbf{Vectorized execution}: PyFlink Vectorized UDFs batch records for SIMD-accelerated evaluation
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Canary Rules.} The six deterministic business rules encode physical impossibility constraints:

\begin{align}
R_1 &: \text{fare\_amount} \geq 0 \quad \text{(negative fare: voided trips, data entry errors)} \label{eq:canary_r1} \\
R_2 &: \text{trip\_distance} > 0 \quad \text{(zero distance: tolls-only, canceled trips)} \label{eq:canary_r2} \\
R_3 &: \text{dropoff\_datetime} > \text{pickup\_datetime} \quad \text{(impossible timestamp: system errors)} \label{eq:canary_r3} \\
R_4 &: \text{speed\_mph} < 100 \quad \text{(GPS/meter malfunction, clipped to physical limits)} \label{eq:canary_r4} \\
R_5 &: \text{fare\_amount} \leq 500 \quad \text{(unreasonably high: meter tampering)} \label{eq:canary_r5} \\
R_6 &: \text{payment\_type} \neq 1 \implies \text{tip\_amount} = 0 \quad \text{(payment inconsistency)} \label{eq:canary_r6}
\end{align}

A record passing all six Canary rules ($R_1 \land R_2 \land R_3 \land R_4 \land R_5 \land R_6$) proceeds to Layer 2. A record failing any rule is immediately routed to the \texttt{dq-hard-rule-violations} topic, bypassing Layer 2 entirely. This early routing reduces average processing latency by 6.75ms per record by avoiding the ML inference pipeline for obviously invalid records \citep{grab_streaming_dq}.

\textbf{Throughput Metrics.} The Canary stage achieves the following throughput characteristics on the evaluation cluster (4 TaskManagers, 8 slots each):

\begin{itemize}
    \item Peak throughput: $\approx$ 50,000 records/second per slot
    \item P99 latency: 2.3ms per record
    \item P99.9 latency: 4.1ms per record
    \item Throughput impact: enables the 16$\times$ improvement achieved by PyFlink Vectorized UDFs (Section 3.3.5)
\end{itemize}

The Canary pattern's design philosophy follows Grab's streaming data quality approach: separate lightweight rules from heavyweight ML inference, using rules to filter obvious violations before expensive computational stages \citep{grab_streaming_dq}.

\subsection{Layer 2: Memory-Based Streaming Anomaly Detection}

Layer 2 implements a sequential two-stage anomaly detection pipeline that processes records through a lightweight Canary (Business Rules) stage followed by a Complex (ML-based) stage, with results converging at the MetaAggregator (detailed in Section 3.3.5). The ML-based anomaly detection combines a Denoising Autoencoder (DAE) with a Memory Module and FIFO buffer to detect multivariate anomalies in data streams without requiring labeled data. This section details the DAE architecture, the memory-based kNN scoring mechanism, the online memory update policy, the 4D context-aware thresholding framework, and the PyFlink implementation.

\subsubsection{Denoising Autoencoder Architecture}

The Online Autoencoder employs a symmetric Denoising Autoencoder architecture that learns robust latent representations of normal taxi trip data. The architecture is: $34$-dimensional input $\rightarrow$ $68$-dimensional hidden layer $\rightarrow$ $34$-dimensional output, with Tanh activation throughout. Default hyperparameters: $k=5$ nearest neighbors, memory buffer $N=8{,}192$ vectors, learning rate $\alpha=0.01$, batch size $1{,}024$, training epochs $5{,}000$ with Adam optimizer.

\textbf{Encoder.} Given an input vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{34}$, the encoder computes a hidden representation $\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^{68}$:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h} = \sigma(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1)
\label{eq:encoder}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{W}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{68 \times 34}$, $\mathbf{b}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{68}$, and $\sigma(x) = \tanh(x)$ is the Tanh activation function.

\textbf{Decoder.} The decoder reconstructs the input from the hidden representation using tied weights ($\mathbf{W}_2 = \mathbf{W}_1^\top$):

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \sigma(\mathbf{W}_1^\top \mathbf{h} + \mathbf{b}_2)
\label{eq:decoder}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{b}_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{34}$. The tied weight constraint halves the number of trainable parameters, reducing the risk of overfitting on the streaming data.

\textbf{Denoising Corruption.} Following Vincent et al. (2010) \citep{vincent2010stacked}, the input $\mathbf{x}$ is corrupted before encoding: $\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$, where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, 0.001^2 \cdot \mathbf{I})$. The corruption std $\sigma_{\text{noise}} = 0.001$ is chosen to be small enough to preserve the underlying structure of normal data but large enough to force the encoder to learn meaningful features rather than the identity function. A denoising autoencoder that simply learned the identity mapping would have $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}$, rendering the latent space trivial and useless for anomaly detection.

\textbf{Loss Function.} The training objective minimizes the mean squared error between the clean input and its reconstruction:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_{\text{data}}}\!\left[\;\|\mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}\|_2^2\;\right]
= \mathbb{E}\!\left[\;\|\mathbf{x} - \text{decoder}(\text{encoder}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}))\|_2^2\;\right]
\label{eq:dae_loss}
\end{equation}

The optimizer is Adam with learning rate $\alpha=10^{-2}$, batch size $1{,}024$, and early stopping on a held-out validation set (10\% of the ultra-clean training corpus). Training converges in approximately $5{,}000$ epochs on the baseline corpus.

\textbf{Why a Denoising Autoencoder for Anomaly Detection?} In the trained DAE, normal records that resemble the training distribution are encoded to a dense manifold in $\mathbb{R}^{34}$ and reconstructed with low error. Anomalous records---which differ from the training distribution---project to regions of the latent space that are far from the normal manifold and are reconstructed with high error. Critically, the Memory Module operates on the encoder output $\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{h}$ (the $68$-dimensional hidden representation) rather than the reconstruction error. This design choice exploits the fact that the encoder learns to separate normal data from anomalies in the latent space, while the decoder's role is confined to training; at inference time, only the encoder is used.

\subsubsection{L1 k-NN Scoring on Memory Module}

The Memory Module maintains a FIFO circular buffer $\mathcal{M}$ of encoded representations of recently observed normal records:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{M} = \left[\mathbf{z}_{t-N+1},\ \mathbf{z}_{t-N+2},\ \ldots,\ \mathbf{z}_t\right],
\quad \mathbf{z}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{34},
\quad N = 8{,}192
\label{eq:memory_buffer}
\end{equation}

Per neighborhood (10 neighborhoods total), the buffer stores $N = 8{,}192$ 34-dimensional encoder outputs. This gives a per-neighborhood memory footprint of $8{,}192 \times 34 \times 4\text{ bytes} \approx 1.1$ MB (assuming float32).

\textbf{L1 (Manhattan) Distance.} For two encoded vectors $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^{34}$, the L1 distance is:

\begin{equation}
R(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) = \|\mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b}\|_1
= \sum_{j=1}^{34} |a_j - b_j|
\label{eq:l1_distance}
\end{equation}

L1 distance is preferred over L2 for high-dimensional anomaly detection: L2 penalizes large per-coordinate deviations quadratically, making it disproportionately sensitive to a single coordinate with a large error; L1 penalizes linearly and preserves coordinate-level interpretability in $d = 34$ dimensions \citep{bhatia2022dense}.

\textbf{k-NN Search.} For each incoming record $\mathbf{x}_t$, the system encodes it to $\mathbf{z}_t$ via the encoder (Equation~\ref{eq:encoder}). It then retrieves the $K = 10$ nearest neighbors $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_1^t, \hat{\mathbf{z}}_2^t, \ldots, \hat{\mathbf{z}}_K^t$ from the memory buffer $\mathcal{M}$ by computing $R(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathbf{z}_i)$ for all $N$ stored vectors and selecting the $K$ with minimum distance. CA-DQStream uses a brute-force scan (optimized via NumPy vectorized operations and BLAS-accelerated matrix-vector products), which is feasible given that $N = 8{,}192$ and each distance computation is 34 additions and comparisons---approximately 1.7 million operations per record, easily handled in sub-millisecond time on modern CPUs.

\textbf{Anomaly Score.} The anomaly score aggregates distances to the $K$ nearest neighbors using a weighted sum:

\begin{equation}
\text{Score}(\mathbf{z}_t) = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \gamma^{\,i-1} \; R\!\big(\mathbf{z}_t,\ \hat{\mathbf{z}}_i^t\big)
= R\!\big(\mathbf{z}_t,\ \hat{\mathbf{z}}_1^t\big)
\quad \text{since } \gamma = 0.0
\label{eq:anomaly_score_v2}
\end{equation}

where $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_i^t$ is the $i$-th nearest neighbor of $\mathbf{z}_t$ at time $t$, and $\gamma \in [0, 1]$ is a geometric decay parameter. With $\gamma = 0.0$, the most recent neighbor (i=1) dominates the score entirely since $\gamma^{i-1} = 0$ for all $i > 1$. This aggressive decay is a deliberate design choice: the most recently observed normal pattern is the strongest indicator of the current concept, and older neighbors provide no additional discriminative power once the most recent one is considered. The score is compared against the per-context threshold $\beta_{\text{ctx}}$ from the 4D threshold matrix; a record is classified as anomalous if:

\begin{equation}
s_{\text{normalized}} = \frac{\text{Score}(\mathbf{z}_t)}{\beta_{\text{ctx}}} \geq 1.0
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad \text{ANOMALY}
\label{eq:anomaly_decision}
\end{equation}

Note: reconstruction error ($\|\mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}\|_2$) is deliberately NOT used in the scoring formula. The anomaly score is purely the sum of L1 distances to the $K$ nearest neighbors in the memory module's latent space. This design was adopted from memstream~\citep{bhatia2022memstream}.

\textbf{What Constitutes an Anomaly.} In CA-DQStream, an anomaly is any record whose k-NN distance score in the memory-augmented latent space significantly exceeds the context-specific normal baseline. Three categories of anomalies are targeted: (1) \textit{infrastructure failures} (GPS errors, meter malfunctions) that produce physically impossible values; (2) \textit{fraud patterns} (meter tampering, short-trip inflation, ratecode misreporting) that individually pass single-feature checks but violate inter-feature relationships; and (3) \textit{distribution shifts} (surge pricing, policy changes) detected at the meta-aggregator level as gradual changes in the quality meta-streams. The system does not distinguish between these categories in the scoring output; all three produce elevated k-NN distances that exceed the per-context threshold.

\subsubsection{Online Memory Update Policy}

The Memory Module uses a conditional FIFO update policy that prevents memory poisoning while enabling continuous adaptation to evolving normal patterns.

\textbf{FIFO Circular Buffer.} The memory buffer is implemented as a circular (ring) buffer of fixed capacity $N = 8{,}192$. A head pointer tracks the oldest entry; on each insert, the head advances modulo $N$, overwriting the oldest entry. At the system's average throughput of approximately $3{,}000{,}000$ records per day, the buffer replaces approximately $3{,}000{,}000 / 8{,}192 \approx 366$ entries per day, meaning the full buffer content is refreshed roughly every 2.7 days under normal operation. This provides $O(1)$ insert and $O(1)$ neighbor search overhead.

\textbf{Conditional Update.} Only records with anomaly score below the per-context threshold are inserted into memory:

\begin{equation}
\text{INSERT}(\mathbf{z}_t, \mathcal{M}) \iff \text{Score}(\mathbf{z}_t) < \beta_{\text{ctx}}
\label{eq:conditional_insert}
\end{equation}

This conditional policy ensures that anomalous records do not contaminate the memory with spurious patterns. Since the threshold $\beta_{\text{ctx}}$ is set at the 95th percentile of normal training scores, approximately 95\% of genuine normal records qualify for insertion, ensuring that the memory buffer is continuously refreshed with current normal patterns.

\textbf{Gradient Detachment.} When encoding $\mathbf{z}_t$, the encoding operation is detached from the autoencoder's gradient graph (via \texttt{z.detach()} in PyTorch or equivalent) before the vector is inserted into the memory buffer. This prevents memory updates from backpropagating gradients into the encoder weights during online operation. The encoder weights remain frozen after the offline training phase; only the memory content is updated online. This architectural choice is critical: if memory updates influenced encoder weights, the autoencoder would gradually adapt to whatever patterns entered memory, creating a feedback loop that could cause catastrophic forgetting or memorization of anomalous patterns.

\textbf{Auto-Update Mechanism.} The memory module implements a continuous refresh cycle driven by the ADWIN-U detector on the anomaly rate meta-stream. When ADWIN-U detects drift (Section 3.3.6), the memory is reset to empty and refilled via FIFO insertion of the next $N = 8{,}192$ records that pass the conditional insert test. This ensures that the memory always represents the most recent $N$ normal records from the current concept, providing a smooth transition when the data distribution shifts.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:memory_update} summarizes the online memory update policy.

\begin{figure}[H]
\caption{Conditional insert into memory buffer based on reconstruction error threshold and context score.}
\label{alg:memory_update}
\begin{lstlisting}[style=pseudocode, language=Python]
# Input: Encoded record z_t in R^34, memory buffer M, context threshold beta_ctx
# Output: Updated memory buffer M'

s = Score(z_t)           # Compute L1 k-NN score (Eq. 4.x)
if s < beta_ctx:
    M.insert(z_t.detach())  # append to FIFO; detach prevents backprop
return M
\end{lstlisting}
\end{figure}

The update policy achieves two objectives simultaneously: it continuously adapts the memory to the current concept (normal patterns evolve over time), and it shields the memory from anomalous patterns (only low-score records enter the buffer). At the system's throughput of approximately 3M records/day, with approximately 95\% of records passing the conditional insert (Section 3.3.3.3), the FIFO buffer refreshes roughly 366 entries per day, replacing its full contents approximately every 2.7 days, ensuring that the stored patterns remain temporally relevant.

\subsubsection{4D Context-Aware Thresholding}

The anomaly score is compared against a context-aware threshold matrix that captures the heterogeneous nature of taxi trip distributions across different operational contexts.

The 4D threshold matrix is indexed by four contextual dimensions: trip\_type $\in \{1,\ldots,7\}$ (RatecodeID categories), time\_window $\in \{1,2,3,4\}$ (night/morning/afternoon/evening), day\_type $\in \{1,2,3\}$ (weekday/weekend/holiday), neighborhood\_id $\in \{1,\ldots,14\}$ (TLC taxi zones), yielding $7 \times 4 \times 3 \times 14 = 1{,}176$ potential cells. Approximately 588 cells have sufficient training samples for reliable threshold estimation.

\textbf{Dimension Selection Rationale.} The four dimensions were selected through an empirical analysis of variance in the baseline anomaly score distribution across candidate contextual factors. Candidate dimensions were ranked by the reduction in score variance they achieved when stratifying by context: neighborhood and temporal window produced the two largest independent reductions, followed by trip type (RatecodeID) and day type (weekday/weekend/holiday). These four dimensions jointly capture the primary structural sources of variance in taxi trip patterns while maintaining sufficient training samples per cell for reliable threshold estimation. Adding a fifth dimension (e.g., weather or traffic conditions) caused minimum cell sizes to drop below a viable threshold, introducing threshold overfitting; dropping any of the four reduced performance on at least one ablation configuration. The 2D production instantiation (neighborhood $\times$ temporal) retains the two highest-impact dimensions and is justified by the empirical variance reduction ranking.

\textbf{Per-Cell Threshold.} Each cell's threshold is the empirical 95th percentile of anomaly scores from the ultra-clean training dataset:

\begin{equation}
T_{\text{cell}} = P_{95}\!\left(\left\{\,\text{Score}(\mathbf{z}_i) : \mathbf{x}_i \in \text{cell}\,\right\}\right)
= \inf\left\{s : \frac{1}{N_{\text{cell}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{cell}}} \mathbf{1}\{\text{Score}(\mathbf{z}_i) \leq s\} \geq 0.95\right\}
\label{eq:threshold_p95}
\end{equation}

This non-parametric definition makes no distributional assumption about the anomaly scores. The 95th percentile naturally targets a 5\% false positive rate per cell: under the training distribution, 95\% of normal records fall below the threshold.

Cells with fewer than 100 training samples fall back to the global 95th percentile of \$45.46, avoiding overfitting threshold estimates to noisy statistics from low-sample cells.

\textbf{Threshold Stability Analysis.} For the NYC TLC dataset, the 4D threshold matrix exhibits significant heterogeneity: airport zones have consistently lower thresholds (mean $T = \$18.3$) due to the tight clustering of airport trip patterns, while low-volume residential zones have higher thresholds (mean $T = \$62.7$) due to the high variance of irregular trip patterns. The global threshold of \$45.46 would produce excessive false positives in airport zones and excessive false negatives in residential zones---the context-aware threshold matrix resolves this by tailoring the sensitivity to each operational context.

\textbf{Normalized Score and Anomaly Decision.} The normalized anomaly score is compared against 1.0: if above, the record is classified as anomalous; otherwise, it is normal. The threshold lookup $T_{\text{cell}}(\mathbf{x}_t)$ is $O(1)$ via a hash table keyed by the 4D context tuple.

\subsubsection{PyFlink Vectorized UDF Implementation}

The Layer 2 ML inference is implemented as PyFlink Vectorized UDFs, which execute Python code on JVM-backed Arrow record batches for high throughput.

\textbf{Batch Processing Pipeline.} Records are accumulated into mini-batches of $B = 500$ records. Each batch is serialized to Arrow format and passed to the Python worker process, where the full inference pipeline (normalize $\rightarrow$ encode $\rightarrow$ kNN score $\rightarrow$ threshold compare) is executed in vectorized NumPy operations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Vectorized Normalization}: $\mathbf{X}_{\text{norm}} = (\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu}) \oslash \boldsymbol{\sigma}$, where $\oslash$ denotes element-wise division. NumPy's broadcasting performs this for all $B$ rows simultaneously.
    \item \textbf{Vectorized Encoding}: $\mathbf{Z} = \sigma(\mathbf{X}_{\text{norm}} \mathbf{W}_1^\top + \mathbf{b}_1)$. The matrix-matrix product $\mathbf{X}_{\text{norm}} \mathbf{W}_1^\top$ is a $(B \times 34) \times (34 \times 68) = B \times 68$ operation accelerated by BLAS.
    \item \textbf{Vectorized kNN}: $\mathbf{D} = \|\mathbf{Z} - \mathcal{M}\|_{1,\text{axis}=1}$ via broadcasting, computing $B \times N$ distances simultaneously.
    \item \textbf{Vectorized Threshold Compare}: $\mathbf{s}_{\text{norm}} = \mathbf{D}_{\text{topK}} / \boldsymbol{\beta}_{\text{ctx}}$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Throughput Improvement.} Vectorized UDFs achieve approximately $16\times$ throughput improvement over scalar Python UDFs by amortizing the JVM-Python inter-process communication (IPC) overhead across 500 records per call, enabling NumPy's BLAS-accelerated linear algebra operations, and utilizing SIMD instructions in the NumPy backend.

\textbf{State Synchronization.} The memory buffer $\mathcal{M}$ is synchronized across all PyFlink worker processes via Flink's Broadcast State mechanism. The broadcast model update message is published to the \texttt{if-model-updates} Kafka topic with a versioned key, ensuring that all workers receive the same model state before the model swap.

\subsection{Layer 3: Meta-Aggregation}

The MetaAggregator constitutes the computational hub of the CA-DQStream pipeline, serving a dual function: it routes records through the downstream storage layer and---critically---generates the quality meta-stream that feeds Layer 4's ADWIN-U drift detection. The key insight is that drift detection operates on meta-metrics (summary statistics of data quality) rather than on raw features, which enables interpretable drift signals that map directly to pipeline components without requiring ground-truth labels. This framing follows the ADWIN-U methodology \citep{adwin2025}.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{ADWIN-U: Drift Detection via Higher-Order Statistics.}

Classical ADWIN \citep{bifet2007learning} maintains a sliding window $W$ and tests whether the means of the older sub-window $W_0$ and the newer sub-window $W_1$ have diverged. The CA-DQStream implementation extends this with ADWIN-U \citep{adwin2025}, which replaces the single mean-comparison test with four independent statistical tests, each targeting a distinct drift type:

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Type 1 --- Mean Shift: Welch's t-test.} A two-sample Welch's t-test compares the means of $W_0$ and $W_1$. Let $n_0 = |W_0|$, $n_1 = |W_1|$, $\bar{x}_0$ and $\bar{x}_1$ the respective sample means, and $s_0^2$, $s_1^2$ the sample variances (ddof=1). The test statistic is:

\begin{equation}
t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_0}{\sqrt{s_0^2 / n_0 + s_1^2 / n_1}}
\label{eq:welch_t_test}
\end{equation}

The degrees of freedom are approximated via the Welch-Satterthwaite formula, and the two-tailed $p$-value is estimated from the standard normal distribution for $df > 30$, or conservatively bounded for smaller samples. Drift is declared when $p < \delta$.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Type 2 --- Variance Shift: Levene's test (Brown-Forsythe).} Levene's test checks whether the spread of $W_0$ and $W_1$ differ, robustly using deviations from the median rather than the mean:

\begin{equation}
W = \frac{(n - 2)\,\sum_{i=1}^{2} n_i\,(\bar{z}_{i} - \bar{z})^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{2}\sum_{j=1}^{n_i}(z_{ij} - \bar{z}_i)^2},
\quad\text{where}\quad z_{ij} = |x_{ij} - \tilde{x}_i|
\label{eq:levene_test}
\end{equation}

where $\tilde{x}_i$ is the median of sub-window $i$, $n = n_0 + n_1$, and $W$ follows a chi-squared distribution under the null. Drift is declared when the approximated $p < \delta$.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Type 3 --- Skewness Shift: Relative difference in skewness.} Skewness ($\gamma_1$, the third standardized moment) measures distributional asymmetry: The detector computes the skewness of the recent sub-window $W_1$ and the older sub-window $W_0$, then compares their relative difference against the Hoeffding threshold $\varepsilon$:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\varepsilon &= \sqrt{\frac{1}{2n}\ln\frac{2}{\delta}}, \\[4pt]
\text{drift} &\iff \frac{|\gamma_1^{(W_1)} - \gamma_1^{(W_0)}|}{|\gamma_1^{(W_0)}| + 10^{-10}} > \varepsilon
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:adwin_hoeffding_skew}
\end{equation}

The window is split as $W_1 = \text{last }\min(n/4, 100)\text{ values}$, $W_0 = W \setminus W_1$.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Type 4 --- Kurtosis Shift: Relative difference in excess kurtosis.} Kurtosis ($\gamma_2$, the fourth standardized moment minus 3, Eq. \ref{eq:kurtosis}) measures tail mass. The same relative-difference test (Eq. \ref{eq:adwin_hoeffding_skew}) is applied to $\gamma_2$, enabling detection of heavy-tail or light-tail shifts that do not affect mean or variance.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Per-metric statistic selection.} ADWIN-U selects the statistical test per metric based on KAIS 2025 rankings: variance for \texttt{volume} (volume spikes manifest as spread changes), skewness for \texttt{null\_rate}, \texttt{violation\_rate}, and \texttt{anomaly\_rate} (rate bursts create asymmetric distributions), and kurtosis for \texttt{avg\_anomaly\_score} and \texttt{delta\_score} (tail-mass changes in score distributions). A complementary secondary statistic cross-check is applied for critical metrics (\texttt{null\_rate}, \texttt{anomaly\_rate}): when primary detects drift, the secondary statistic (e.g., skewness primary $\rightarrow$ kurtosis secondary) must also indicate change, reducing false positives.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Window management.} The ADWIN-U window stores up to 500 scalar meta-metric values (via a deque with \texttt{maxlen}=500). Drift checking begins after the window accumulates at least 50 values (\texttt{min\_window\_size}=50). Upon drift declaration, the window shrinks to retain only the recent half $W_1$, discarding $W_0$ as stale. In CA-DQStream, one ADWIN-U instance monitors each of the six meta-metrics per neighborhood (60 instances total), with independent drift detection and independent window management.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Higher-Order Statistics: Skewness and Kurtosis.}

Skewness ($\gamma_1$) and excess kurtosis ($\gamma_2$) are non-parametric sufficient statistics that jointly characterize distributional shape beyond first- and second-order moments. Two distributions with identical mean and variance may differ dramatically in their higher-order structure: a unimodal distribution can become bimodal, a symmetric distribution can develop a heavy tail, or a light-tailed distribution can acquire extreme outliers. DTD theory \citep{dtd2026} establishes that skewness and kurtosis are sufficient for distribution comparison under mild regularity conditions and provide discriminative power beyond mean and variance for detecting distributional changes relevant to anomaly detection performance.

Skewness measures the asymmetry of a distribution around its mean:

\begin{equation}
\gamma_1 = \frac{E\big[(X - \mu)^3\big]}{\sigma^3}
        = \frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^3}
               {\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2\right)^{3/2}}
\label{eq:skewness}
\end{equation}

For a symmetric distribution (e.g., the Gaussian), $\gamma_1 = 0$. A positive skewness ($\gamma_1 > 0$) indicates a right-skewed distribution with an extended upper tail; negative skewness indicates left skew. In streaming DQ, a sudden increase in skewness of the anomaly score distribution signals that the ML model is encountering records with systematically higher reconstruction errors---potentially a new anomaly subtype or a distributional shift.

Excess kurtosis measures tail mass relative to the Gaussian:

\begin{equation}
\gamma_2 = \frac{E\big[(X - \mu)^4\big]}{\sigma^4} - 3
        = \frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^4}
               {\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2\right)^{2}} - 3
\label{eq:kurtosis}
\end{equation}

The subtraction of $3$ yields excess kurtosis, so that the Gaussian has $\gamma_2 = 0$. Positive excess kurtosis ($\gamma_2 > 0$, leptokurtic) indicates heavy tails and a sharp peak; negative excess kurtosis ($\gamma_2 < 0$, platykurtic) indicates light tails. In CA-DQStream, kurtosis of the violation rate distribution serves as an early warning: a sudden increase signals that extreme violation spikes are becoming more frequent, potentially driven by upstream data capture failures.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Six Meta-Metric Definitions.}

The MetaAggregator computes six meta-metrics over 1-minute tumbling windows, each targeting a specific dimension of data quality. All metrics are aggregated per neighborhood to preserve spatial context. These six meta-metrics feed the ADWIN-U drift detector in Layer 4:

\begin{align}
\text{null\_rate}(t) &= \frac{1}{n_t^{\text{total}}}\,
                           \sum_{i=1}^{n_t^{\text{total}}} \mathbf{1}\{x_i^{\text{null}} = \text{TRUE}\}
                           \;\;\in\;[0, 1] \label{eq:null_rate_v2} \\[6pt]
\text{violation\_rate}(t) &= \frac{1}{n_t^{\text{total}}}\,
                              \sum_{i=1}^{n_t^{\text{total}}} \mathbf{1}\{x_i^{\text{BR}} = \text{VIOLATED}\}
                              \;\;\in\;[0, 1] \label{eq:violation_rate_v2} \\[6pt]
\text{anomaly\_rate}(t) &= \frac{1}{n_t^{\text{total}}}\,
                            \sum_{i=1}^{n_t^{\text{total}}} \mathbf{1}\{\text{Score}_i \geq \beta_{\text{ctx}}\}
                            \;\;\in\;[0, 1] \label{eq:anomaly_rate_v2} \\[6pt]
\text{volume}(t) &= n_t^{\text{total}} \quad \text{(records per 1-min window)} \label{eq:volume_v2} \\[6pt]
\text{avg\_anomaly\_score}(t) &= \frac{1}{n_t^{\text{anom}}}\,
                                   \sum_{i=1}^{n_t^{\text{anom}}} \text{Score}_i
                                   \quad (n_t^{\text{anom}} \geq 1) \label{eq:avg_anomaly_score_v2} \\[6pt]
\Delta\_\text{score}(t) &= \frac{\big|\,\text{violation\_rate}(t) - \text{anomaly\_rate}(t)\,\big|}
                               {\text{violation\_rate}(t) + \text{anomaly\_rate}(t) + \epsilon}
                          \;\;\in\;[0, 1] \label{eq:delta_score_v2}
\end{align}

where $n_t^{\text{total}}$ is the total record count in window $t$, $\mathbf{1}\{\cdot\}$ is the indicator function, $\beta_{\text{ctx}}$ is the per-context 4D threshold from Section 3.3.4, and $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$ is a smoothing constant preventing division by zero. The six metrics (null\_rate, violation\_rate, anomaly\_rate, volume, avg\_anomaly\_score, $\Delta$\_score) are emitted as a single record to the \texttt{dq-meta-stream} Kafka topic per neighborhood per window. The Flink operator groups records by \texttt{neighborhood\_id} (10 neighborhoods) before applying the 1-minute tumbling window, ensuring that meta-metrics are spatially contextualized.

A high weighted score indicates systemic drift requiring \texttt{quick\_retrain}. The ADWIN-U detector operates independently on each of the six meta-streams; the drift aggregator's role is to combine these independent signals into a single severity assessment.

\subsection{Layer 4: Intelligent Evolution Controller}

The Intelligent Evolution Controller (IEC) provides autonomous drift handling for CA-DQStream by monitoring the quality meta-streams from Layer 3, detecting statistical distribution shifts via ADWIN-U, and triggering model retraining when necessary. The IEC is the system's self-healing layer: it closes the feedback loop from quality degradation back to model adaptation, ensuring that the ML model remains accurate as the underlying data distribution evolves.

The IEC operates in a continuous observe-decide-act loop on each MetaAggregator 1-minute window. It receives six meta-metrics (volume, null\_rate, violation\_rate, anomaly\_rate, avg\_anomaly\_score, delta\_score) per neighborhood, updates per-neighborhood ADWIN-U instances, assesses drift severity, and selects between two strategies: \texttt{do\_nothing} or \texttt{quick\_retrain}. The latter writes a signed retrain signal to MinIO for the ML service to execute model retraining.

\subsubsubsection{IEC Architecture: Three Core Components}

The IEC is composed of three tightly integrated components:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MultiInstanceADWIN}: Maintains one ADWIN-U instance per neighborhood $\times$ metric combination (60 instances for 10 neighborhoods $\times$ 6 metrics). Each ADWIN-U instance detects drift in its respective meta-stream with per-metric $\delta$ (Table~\ref{tab:adwin_params_v2}) and reports the drift type (mean, variance, skewness, or kurtosis shift).
    \item \textbf{DriftAggregator}: Aggregates drift events from all ADWIN-U instances and assesses overall severity using a weighted score: skewness shifts (weight 2.0) and kurtosis shifts (weight 1.8) carry higher diagnostic weight than variance shifts (weight 1.2) and mean shifts (weight 1.0). Severity levels: none (weighted score = 0), low (< 3.0), moderate (3.0--8.0), high ($\geq$ 8.0). Only high severity triggers \texttt{quick\_retrain}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsubsection{IEC Decision Outcomes}

The IEC Phase 3 pipeline produces two possible decision outcomes, which select from four underlying adaptation strategies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{do\_nothing} (decision outcome): No drift detected or severity below threshold. The system continues normal operation. This is the default outcome for the vast majority of windows.
    \item \textbf{quick\_retrain} (decision outcome): Severe drift detected. The IEC writes a retrain signal to object storage, which the ML service consumes to trigger model recalibration, dispatching one of four adaptation strategies (Continuous Evolution, Switching Scheme, METER Adaptation, or Spatial Tracking) based on drift characteristics.
\end{itemize}

The two decision outcomes are severity-based: only \texttt{high} severity (weighted score $\geq$ 8.0) triggers the \texttt{quick\_retrain} outcome; all lower severity levels map to \texttt{do\_nothing}. The \texttt{quick\_retrain} outcome selects among four adaptation strategies (Continuous Evolution 78\%, Switching Scheme 15\%, METER Adaptation 6\%, Spatial Tracking 1\%) based on the dominant drift pattern, as validated in the experiment results (Section 5.2.3). This conservative severity threshold prevents over-retraining on transient fluctuations while ensuring heavy drift is addressed by full adaptation.

\textbf{Circuit Breaker Integration.} After executing \texttt{quick\_retrain}, the circuit breaker records the action. Only actual adaptations (not \texttt{do\_nothing}) count toward the consecutive-action counter. If a retrain succeeds, the kNN baseline is reset to recalculate from the new memory state. If the ML service fails, the breaker records a failure and trips after three consecutive failures.

\subsubsubsection{Three Retrain Triggers}

The \texttt{quick\_retrain} strategy is forced regardless of severity when any of three hard triggers fires. These triggers are evaluated per neighborhood on every MetaAggregator window:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Trigger A (ADWIN Drift):} ADWIN has detected drift at least once within the monitoring window for this neighborhood (fires on the first drift event, not after 7+ consecutive days).
    \item \textbf{Trigger B (Anomaly Rate):} Anomaly rate exceeds 15\% on any occurrence. This trigger fires when the ML model's false-negative rate increases, indicating the model has fallen behind the current concept.
    \item \textbf{Trigger C (kNN Distance):} The average kNN distance in the memory buffer exceeds 2$\times$ the baseline kNN distance computed from the first 50 post-retrain records. This trigger fires when the memory buffer's internal coherence degrades, indicating that stored patterns no longer resemble incoming records.
\end{enumerate}

When any trigger fires, the IEC overrides the severity-based strategy with \texttt{quick\_retrain} at confidence 0.95, logs the trigger details, and writes the retrain signal to MinIO. The affected neighborhood list is derived from the drift aggregator's affected neighborhoods.

The $\Delta$\_score meta-metric (defined in Eq. \ref{eq:delta_score_v2} from Section 3.3.5) is included in the 6D meta-metric vector fed to ADWIN for monitoring. A sudden increase in $\Delta$\_score indicates that Canary and the Complex stage are disagreeing more frequently, which may signal emerging drift --- though it is not a direct retrain trigger in the Phase 3 implementation.

\subsubsubsection{ADWIN-U Drift Detection in the IEC}

Each ADWIN-U instance monitors one of the six meta-metrics per neighborhood independently. On each update, the instance splits its sliding window $W$ into a recent sub-window $W_1$ (last $\min(|W|/4, 100)$ values) and an older sub-window $W_0$. It computes the per-metric primary statistic (skewness for null\_rate/violation\_rate/anomaly\_rate, kurtosis for avg\_anomaly\_score/delta\_score, variance for volume) on both sub-windows and applies the corresponding statistical test. Drift is declared when the test exceeds the per-metric $\delta$ threshold (Table~\ref{tab:adwin_params_v2}); the window then shrinks to retain only $W_1$. ADWIN-U instances are reset independently per neighborhood.

\subsubsubsection{Verification Feedback Loop (Phase 4 Reserved)}

The \texttt{VerificationFeedbackLoop} class is implemented but reserved for Phase 4 integration. Its algorithm compares post-retrain metrics against pre-retrain baselines over a 3-window verification period. If consecutive adaptations fail to improve the anomaly rate, the loop escalates (after 3 failures) and ultimately triggers rollback (after 5 failures). Phase 3 uses the circuit breaker as the primary protection against adaptation storms, deferring verification-based rollback to future work.
