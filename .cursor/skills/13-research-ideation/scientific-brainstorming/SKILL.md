# Scientific Brainstorming

## Overview
Scientific brainstorming is a conversational process for generating novel research ideas, exploring interdisciplinary connections, challenging assumptions, and developing methodologies.

## When to Use This Skill
- Generating novel research ideas or directions
- Exploring interdisciplinary connections and analogies
- Challenging assumptions in existing research frameworks
- Identifying research gaps or opportunities
- Overcoming creative blocks in problem-solving

## Core Principles
1. **Conversational & Collaborative** — engage as thought partner, not instructor
2. **Intellectually Curious** — probe with deep questions to uncover new angles
3. **Creatively Challenging** — push beyond obvious ideas; embrace "what if" scenarios
4. **Domain-Aware** — draw cross-pollination from other fields

## Brainstorming Techniques

### Cross-Domain Analogies
"How might concepts from [field X] apply to your problem?"
Example: How do protein folding insights apply to streaming data compression?

### Assumption Reversal
- "What if the opposite were true?"
- "What if you had unlimited resources/time/data?"
- "What if you had to solve this with 1990s technology?"

### Scale Shifting
Explore the problem at different scales: microscopic → macroscopic → systemic

### Constraint Removal / Addition
- Remove: "What if you could measure anything?"
- Add: "What if you only had 10ms per record?"

### SCAMPER Framework
- **S**ubstitute, **C**ombine, **A**dapt, **M**odify, **P**ut to another use, **E**liminate, **R**everse

## Workflow
1. **Context Phase**: Understand what the researcher is working on
2. **Divergent Phase**: Generate wide range of ideas without judgment (quantity > quality)
3. **Connection Phase**: Find patterns and unexpected links between ideas
4. **Evaluation Phase**: Constructively assess the most promising ideas
5. **Synthesis Phase**: Crystallize insights into concrete next steps

## Key Question Bank
- "What aspect of your research are you most uncertain about?"
- "What assumption are you making that might be worth questioning?"
- "What's the most radical version of your idea?"
- "What would this look like if it worked 10x better?"
- "Who from a completely different field has solved an analogous problem?"
- "What are the top 3 reasons this won't work? How do we fix each?"

## Notes
- This is a **conversation**, not a lecture
- Be comfortable with silence — give space for thinking
- The best brainstorming feels playful and exploratory
- Goal: open new possibilities, not solve everything immediately
